**Idea 1: ShootScore**

**Problem:** Competitive pistol and rifle shooters (USPSA, IDPA, 3-Gun) generate match result data through Practiscore, but Practiscore is match *management* software, not a personal analytics tool. After a match, a shooter can see their rank and score, but they can't answer questions like: "Is my accuracy getting worse as stage time pressure increases?" or "Which classifier type am I consistently underperforming in vs. my classification level?" Serious competitors track this in hand-maintained spreadsheets, which means almost no one does it consistently. They spend $1,500+ on custom gear to shave 0.05 seconds off a draw and then leave concrete performance data sitting unused.

**Target buyer:** Classified USPSA/IDPA competitors (B-class and above) who shoot 10+ matches per year and are actively trying to improve their division ranking — not casual range-goers.

**Monetization:** $6/month or $49/year subscription. Competitive shooters are proven spenders with strong opinions and active online communities. At 400 annual subscribers, that's ~$20K ARR. Small but ramen-profitable for a side project with near-zero infrastructure cost.

**MVP scope:** Import Practiscore CSV exports (the format is public and consistent). Display classifier history with trendlines, hit factor over time by stage type, accuracy vs. speed scatter plots, and a simple "vs. classification threshold" bar. Mobile-friendly web app — no native app required yet. Does NOT integrate with shot timer apps, does NOT do video analysis, does NOT have social/leaderboard features.

**Distribution:** Post detailed "here's what my classifier data actually shows" analysis threads in r/competitionshooting, r/USPSA, and the Brian Enos Forums (the main serious competitor forum). These communities reward data-driven content. Offer free import for 90 days, then soft paywall. Sponsor one mid-size match in your region for $200 in prize table credit — the announcer mentions you to 80+ shooters at once.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Practiscore is focused on match directors, not individual shooters. No major sports analytics company is touching a niche this small. The Practiscore CSV format is simple enough to parse in an afternoon. The community is tight-knit and word-of-mouth travels fast once one respected shooter endorses it publicly.

**Biggest risk:** Practiscore changes their export format or builds this feature themselves. Mitigate by making the import engine modular and adding manual entry as a fallback.

**Idea 2: SampleVault**

**Problem:** Bedroom producers accumulate sample libraries of 20,000–100,000 audio files across years of Splice subscriptions, free pack downloads, and personal recordings. These files live in chaotic folder hierarchies with names like `kick_808_final_v2_USE THIS.wav`. Every major DAW has a mediocre browser. Third-party tools like XO or Serato Sample are either DAW-specific, subscription-locked, or only work with their own curated content. The actual pain: producers spend 30 minutes hunting for the right snare before giving up and using the one they always use. Discovery of their *own* library is broken.

**Target buyer:** Bedroom producers who release on Bandcamp or SoundCloud, have an active Splice subscription or large local sample library, and work on a Mac or Windows machine — not enterprise studios with full-time sound designers.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase, $49 (with a free tier capped at 5,000 files). Estimated 1,500 sales in year one from organic music production community reach = ~$73K gross. A small recurring "cloud sync" add-on at $4/month for producers who work across machines adds a long-tail revenue stream.

**MVP scope:** A desktop app (Python + Electron wrapper or Tauri) that scans a local folder, analyzes each audio file using `librosa` for BPM and key detection, classifies instrument type via a lightweight local ML model (essentia or a fine-tuned ONNX model), and stores results in a local SQLite database. UI: waveform preview on hover, tag-based search, drag-to-DAW. Does NOT sync to cloud, does NOT integrate with DAW plugins, does NOT touch samples on Splice's servers.

**Distribution:** YouTube tutorials showing "I organized my entire 80,000 sample library in 4 hours" — this format performs extremely well in the music production creator ecosystem. Post the before/after in r/edmproduction and r/WeAreTheMusicMakers. Reach out to 5–10 mid-tier music production YouTubers (50K–500K subs) for affiliate deals (20% cut per referred sale).

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The big players (Native Instruments, iZotope) want to sell you their sample content, not help you manage samples you bought elsewhere — it conflicts with their business model. A solo dev has no such conflict and can position explicitly as "works with everything, owns nothing."

**Biggest risk:** Librosa's BPM and key detection is imprecise enough on some genres (lo-fi, ambient) that producers dismiss the product as inaccurate. Mitigate by letting users manually correct tags and making "untagged" a useful browseable state rather than a failure mode.

**Idea 3: StringSync**

**Problem:** An indie game dev ships their Steam game in English, then gets translations from community volunteers or cheap freelancers for 5 more languages. Six months later they release a content update that changes 40 strings, adds 80 new ones, and rewrites 15 others. Now every translation file is in an unknown state of staleness — nobody knows which lines are new, which were changed enough to matter, and which are fine. The dev either skips updating the translations (breaking non-English players) or manually diffs files in VSCode for hours. This exact situation is described in steam developer forums and on r/gamedev constantly.

**Target buyer:** Solo or 2-person indie dev teams self-publishing on Steam or itch.io, who've shipped at least one game and are managing 3+ language translation files — not AAA studios with dedicated localization pipelines.

**Monetization:** $19/month per project (unlimited languages), or $149/year. A free tier allows one project with up to 2 languages. At 150 paying project subscriptions, that's ~$34K ARR. This is a "small recurring pain with professional buyers" — exactly the subscription sweet spot.

**MVP scope:** A CLI tool (`stringsync diff`, `stringsync report`, `stringsync push`) that compares a source language file (JSON, CSV, or Gettext PO) against each translation file, outputs a structured "needs translation" manifest (new strings, changed strings beyond a similarity threshold, deleted strings), and generates a formatted brief you can send directly to a translator. Optional DeepL API integration to pre-fill machine translation drafts for human review. Web dashboard shows per-language completion percentage. Does NOT auto-push to Steam. Does NOT manage translator payments or hiring.

**Distribution:** Post a detailed "how I manage my translations without going insane" writeup on the Steam developer forums, r/gamedev, and Gamasutra/Game Developer. The writeup teaches the workflow manually — the CLI is the shortcut. Offer a GitHub repo for the free tier to build trust with technical devs before asking for payment on the dashboard.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Enterprise localization platforms (Lokalise, Crowdin) start at $120–$200/month and are built for teams. A solo game dev doesn't need role-based access control and a vendor marketplace — they need a CLI that fits into their existing Git workflow and costs less than a single Steam key pack.

**Biggest risk:** The free-tier CLI gets forked and used without conversion to paid. Mitigate by making the web dashboard (completion tracking, translator-facing review UI, version history) the real value anchor, not the CLI diff itself.

**Idea 4: ChronicPaw**

**Problem:** Cats with chronic conditions — kidney disease (CKD affects ~30% of cats over 10), hyperthyroidism, and feline diabetes — require daily medication, specific diet monitoring, and frequent bloodwork. Owners track this in a combination of phone notes, spreadsheets, and memory. The real pain surfaces at vet appointments: the vet asks "has she been drinking more water this week?" and the owner has no data. Or a cat is on three medications with different twice-daily schedules and the owner genuinely can't remember if the morning dose happened. Existing pet health apps are generic, designed around vaccination reminders and single-pet households, and don't model chronic disease management.

**Target buyer:** Cat owners (skewing female, 35–60) who have one or more cats actively being treated for a chronic condition and whose annual vet spend exceeds $1,000 — a group with proven willingness to pay for their cat's quality of life.

**Monetization:** $3.99/month or $29.99/year. The key is the iOS/Android App Store as distribution, where the $3.99/month price point is impulse-level for someone already spending $200/month on prescription food and medication. At 800 annual subscribers = ~$24K ARR. A one-time "vet export PDF" unlock at $4.99 serves users who don't want a subscription.

**MVP scope:** React Native app (ships to both stores from one codebase). Core features: medication tracking with flexible schedules and "did both of us give this dose?" confirmation, daily symptom log (water intake, appetite, litter box output — the exact metrics vets ask about), bloodwork result entry with trend graph over time, and one-tap export of a clean 1-page PDF summary for vet visits. Supports up to 3 cats. Does NOT integrate with vet EMR systems, does NOT have community features, does NOT do any diagnostic inference.

**Distribution:** Facebook groups for CKD cats and hyperthyroid cats are enormous (30,000–100,000 members) and extremely active. A genuine post with a real sick cat story and a link to the app will spread without paid promotion. Partner with 2–3 "cat health" micro-influencers on Instagram who document caring for sick cats. These creators need content; a useful app is content.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** This niche is too small for a VC-backed company to bother with, and too emotionally specific for a generic pet app to serve well. The product's legitimacy comes from visibly understanding the actual vocabulary of CKD management — creatinine levels, phosphorus binders, subQ fluids — which a solo dev who knows the community can encode authentically.

**Biggest risk:** User acquisition is almost entirely organic and community-dependent. If the Facebook group moderators are hostile or the first community post lands wrong, growth stalls hard. Mitigate by showing up in the community for 4–6 weeks before posting the product.

**Idea 5: Parsec Station**

**Problem:** Authors and tabletop RPG game masters running hard science fiction campaigns (The Expanse RPG, Traveller, homebrew settings) obsess over internal consistency — travel times between star systems, communication lag across a solar system, how many years a relativistic journey ages the crew versus people on the homeworld. Generic worldbuilding tools like WorldAnvil or Obsidian vaults don't model any of this; they're text databases. The result is either embarrassing continuity errors in published fiction ("that trip took 3 weeks but the message arrived in 2 days?") or hours of manual math that breaks creative flow. This is a loud, unsolved complaint in hard SF writing communities.

**Target buyer:** Hard SF authors (self-published and traditionally published) and game masters running physics-respecting sci-fi campaigns — people who have already bought books on orbital mechanics for writers and who cite "the math" as a point of pride in their setting.

**Monetization:** $9/month or $79/year. This is a "hobbyist with professional aspirations" market — self-pub authors who make $500–$5,000/year from their fiction will still pay $9/month for a tool that makes their setting tighter. At 300 subscribers = $32K ARR. A one-time "worldbook export" (PDF or HTML of your entire setting) at $14.99 per export serves occasional users.

**MVP scope:** A web app with four interconnected tools: (1) **Star System Builder** — place stars and planets, input orbital parameters, get calculated travel times at 1g constant acceleration with flip-and-burn turnaround; (2) **Communications Lag Calculator** — given two bodies' current orbital positions, calculate light-lag for any date in your timeline; (3) **Relativistic Journey Log** — input journey parameters, get shipboard time vs. station time delta; (4) **Timeline** — a visual timeline where you can place events, characters' ages auto-update based on their relativistic travel history. Does NOT generate prose, does NOT do alien language generation, does NOT have multiplayer collaboration yet.

**Distribution:** Post in r/HardSciFi, r/worldbuilding, r/traveller (the RPG), and r/scifiwriting with a genuinely interesting "I built a tool that does the Expanse math for you" demo. Write a guest post for a hard SF author newsletter (several have 5K–20K subscribers). The audience will self-identify loudly because this is a pain they've complained about publicly and repeatedly.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The intersection of "understands relativistic physics at a useful level" AND "can ship a web app" is rare. WorldAnvil has 500,000 users but they're almost all fantasy writers — hard SF is an afterthought. A focused tool that speaks the community's language (and gets the math right) wins by specificity. There is no serious competitor here; the main competitor is a Python script someone wrote for themselves and never shipped.

**Biggest risk:** The audience is passionate but small, and their willingness to pay for *software* (versus books or sourcebooks) is unproven. Run a $0 waitlist with a feature-complete demo before charging. If you can't get 200 waitlist signups from community posts, the market is too small.
