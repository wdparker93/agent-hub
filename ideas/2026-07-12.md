# Weekly App Ideas — July 12, 2026

**Idea 1: Handload Ledger**

**Problem:** Handloaders (people who reload their own ammunition) are meticulous hobbyists who track dozens of variables per load recipe — powder type, charge weight, bullet, primer, brass, overall cartridge length, velocity from chronograph sessions, and cost per round. Almost all of them do this in spreadsheets or aging Windows-only software that looks like it was designed in 2003 (because it was). When a recipe works, finding it later, cloning it for a new caliber, or comparing two loads from different range sessions requires digging through ugly tables with no visual feedback. The good old tools like LoadBook or QuickLOAD handle ballistics calculation but nothing handles *recipe lifecycle management* — the act of developing, refining, archiving, and costing out loads across multiple firearms over years.

**Target buyer:** Centerfire handloaders who reload for precision rifle, USPSA/IPSC competition, or big-bore hunting — typically 35–60 year old men with a dedicated reloading bench, $1,000+ in equipment, and no patience for jank. They're not price-sensitive on useful tools.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase, $34.99. Desktop app (Windows primary, Mac secondary via Python/Tauri or Electron). Optional $4.99/month sync tier for cross-device backup and cloud export to PDF range cards. Even 500 sales/year = $17,500. Reloading forums have hundreds of thousands of active users; this is a real niche.

**MVP scope:** Create and save load recipes (caliber, components, charge weight, OAL). Log range sessions with velocity strings (manual entry or CSV import from common chronographs like MagnetoSpeed/Labradar). Auto-calculate SD/ES, cost per round based on component prices you set. Search and filter your recipe library. Export a clean PDF "range card." Does NOT do external ballistics calculation, does NOT integrate with any external ballistics APIs, no mobile app yet.

**Distribution:** Post in r/reloading, The High Road, Sniper's Hide forums, and Accurate Shooter. Record a YouTube walkthrough showing you loading a .308 recipe and logging a range session. One post in the right forum thread ("I built this because I was tired of spreadsheets") will drive the first 100 customers. Reloaders share tools they actually use.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Hornady and RCBS make the equipment; they don't make workflow software. The existing software has zero design sensibility — it's engineered, not designed. A solo dev who shoots can build something that *feels right* to reloaders in a way a product team at a brass manufacturer never will. The community is also deeply word-of-mouth-driven inside its own forums, which a big company can't fake.

**Biggest risk:** The audience skews older and some are genuinely fine with their spreadsheets. The validation question is whether "it would be nicer" translates to "I'll pay $35." Solve this by posting a free beta first and watching whether people actually log their loads daily or abandon it after one session.

**Idea 2: DAW Project Surgeon**

**Problem:** Music producers accumulate thousands of DAW project files over years, and every project is a tangled web of references — samples scattered across drives, third-party loops in a folder that moved, plugin presets saved somewhere in AppData. When you try to revisit an old project, archive your work, migrate to a new machine, or figure out *which* sample from your 80GB library you used on that track, there is no tool that helps. Ableton has a basic "Collect All and Save" feature; Logic has a similar function. Both are brittle, all-or-nothing, and give you zero visibility into what's actually happening before you commit. The underlying problem — mapping the real dependency graph of a project folder — is an automation problem, not a music problem.

**Target buyer:** Bedroom producers who've been making music for 3+ years in Ableton, FL Studio, or Logic, have multiple external drives of samples, and have felt the panic of opening a project on a new machine to find 60% of the samples missing. Also targets producers who sell beats or sample packs and need to audit their own work before distribution.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase $24.99 via direct download (Gumroad or own site). Target Ableton users first because their project format (`.als` is compressed XML) is parseable without an official API. Upsell a $9.99/year "live monitoring" tier that watches a folder and alerts on broken references as they happen. 1,000 sales/year is extremely achievable in this community = $25K/year.

**MVP scope:** Point the app at a folder of Ableton `.als` files. It parses them, extracts every sample reference path, checks whether the file exists at that path, and shows you a clear report: green (found), yellow (found at a different path via search), red (missing). One-click "collect missing files" attempts to locate them by filename across all mounted drives. Export a manifest CSV. Does NOT support FL Studio or Logic yet, does NOT move or reorganize files automatically, no plugin dependency tracking.

**Distribution:** r/ableton, r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, Ableton-focused YouTube tutorials, and the Ableton Forum. This solves a problem that gets posted about multiple times per week ("old project, missing samples, help"). Drop a free beta in those threads. Producer YouTubers love to cover utility tools that solve real problems.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Ableton as a company has no incentive to build deep project archaeology tooling — it doesn't help them sell licenses. No VC-backed startup is going to build a one-time-purchase $25 desktop utility for a niche workflow problem. The format is technically accessible enough that a solo Python developer can ship a working parser in a weekend. The community trust dynamic also favors someone who's clearly a producer building for producers.

**Biggest risk:** Ableton changes their project file format in a major release and the parser breaks. Mitigate by targeting the last 2-3 stable format versions and building a format-version detection layer early.

**Idea 3: LoopKit**

**Problem:** Bedroom producers who want to license their music to indie games face a specific, frustrating packaging problem: game engines don't play audio the way Spotify does. A track needs a precisely defined loop point so it cycles without a pop or silence. Stems need to be exported at consistent RMS levels for the game audio engine to balance them. License documentation needs to say specific things for a developer to actually use it legally and credit you. Most producers either never think about this and get rejected by game devs who don't know what to do with their files, or spend 3 hours per track fighting with Audacity and a template Word doc. The gap between "I made a great track" and "I packaged this for game use" is unsexy, technical, and currently solved by nothing.

**Target buyer:** Bedroom producers who release lo-fi, chiptune, ambient, or synth music and are either selling directly to indie devs on itch.io/Discord or listing on marketplaces like GameDevMarket. They've already made the music — they want the last-mile tooling to monetize it properly.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase $29 desktop app or web tool. Could also do a per-export credit model ($0.50/track exported) if users only package occasionally. Producers who are seriously trying to license will pay for anything that saves them 2 hours per release.

**MVP scope:** Drag in an audio file. Set loop start and end points with a visual waveform editor that snaps to zero-crossings (prevents pops). Preview the loop playing continuously. Export: looped WAV, intro WAV (pre-loop section), a basic stem naming convention if you drag in multiples. Generate a simple plain-language license PDF (you fill in the price tier: free/attribution, commercial, exclusive). Does NOT do audio mastering, does NOT connect to any marketplace, no DAW plugin.

**Distribution:** itch.io producer community, r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, Splice community forums, YouTube tutorials from indie game dev channels that cover "where to get music." The pitch writes itself: "Turn your tracks into game-ready audio packs in 10 minutes."

**Why a solo dev wins here:** This is an intersection problem — it requires knowing music production *and* knowing how game audio works. Neither the Ableton ecosystem nor the Unity ecosystem has any incentive to build this bridge. It's too small for a middleware company and too technical for a marketplace to bother with. A developer who understands both worlds can ship something opinionated and actually useful in weeks.

**Biggest risk:** The market size on the selling-producer side might be smaller than it looks. Most bedroom producers don't actively license — they think about it but don't pursue it. Need to validate whether there's a real active pipeline of "producers who are currently trying to sell to game devs" versus "producers who like the idea."

**Idea 4: SteamLens**

**Problem:** Indie game developers spend months building a game, then treat their Steam store page as an afterthought and wonder why their launch week numbers are brutal. The problem isn't the game — it's that their tags are wrong, their short description doesn't match what players actually search for, and their capsule art signals the wrong genre. Steam's algorithm is heavily tag and engagement-driven, but there's no tool that shows you: here's what the top 20 games in your sub-genre actually do with their tags, here's which keywords appear in descriptions of games with similar review counts, here's the price distribution for your market segment, here's what your page is missing. SteamDB shows raw data; it doesn't synthesize it into actionable page-level recommendations for a specific game.

**Target buyer:** Solo indie developers and two-person studios who are 3–6 months from shipping on Steam and know their discoverability is a problem. Also useful for devs who already shipped and are trying to understand why their page isn't converting. These people spend real money on assets, marketing, and tools — a $50 report or a $20/month subscription is trivial if it moves the needle on discovery.

**Monetization:** $19/month SaaS or $49 one-time "analysis report" per game. The one-time model is actually stronger here — a developer pays once before launch, gets a deep report, acts on it. Could do both: report tier and ongoing monitoring subscription. 100 paying customers/month at $19 = $1,900 MRR. Modest but real, and the audience is highly targetable.

**MVP scope:** User enters their Steam App ID (or a game they're planning, modeled after a comparable). App scrapes public Steam store data (legal, public-facing) for the top 50 games in matched tags. Outputs: tag frequency analysis across comparables, price distribution histogram, review-to-wishlists ratio benchmarks, keyword frequency in short descriptions, and a simple checklist of "your page vs. the benchmark." No AI writing of store copy, no API integration with Steam partner tools, no actual Steam account connection.

**Distribution:** r/gamedev, r/indiegaming, the Indie Game Developers Facebook group, Game Dev Unlocked podcast listeners, and — critically — this tool is directly relevant to the indie game dev YouTube ecosystem (channels like Thomas Brush, Jonas Tyroller). One mention in a relevant YouTube video drives significant traffic. Launch on Product Hunt in the "developer tools" category.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Valve has zero incentive to give developers this kind of benchmarking visibility. Big analytics companies target AAA publishers. The indie dev community is underserved by serious data tooling and has learned to pay for things that save them time or improve their odds. A solo dev who is also a gamer will frame this exactly the way indie devs think about their own situation.

**Biggest risk:** Steam's scraping policies could tighten, or they could start rate-limiting aggressively. Mitigate by caching data intelligently, queuing requests slowly, and only scraping public-facing pages — not anything behind Steam partner auth. The other risk: developers optimize their page but it doesn't actually move sales, and they blame the tool.

**Idea 5: Catstack**

**Problem:** People with three or more cats — and there are millions of them — are managing genuinely complex per-cat logistics: different feeding schedules (one cat is diabetic, one is on a urinary diet, one free-feeds), different medication schedules (insulin twice daily, thyroid pill in food, monthly flea prevention), multiple vet relationships, and weight trends that vets actually ask about at every checkup. General pet apps treat all pets as interchangeable and focus on reminders for one or two animals. The specific pain of *multi-cat household management* — knowing at a glance that you've given today's insulin to Mochi but not Biscuit, that Biscuit is due for a recheck in 11 days, and that Ghost has been losing weight for three months — is completely unaddressed by anything that currently exists.

**Target buyer:** Cat owners with 3+ cats, particularly those managing one or more cats with chronic health conditions (diabetes, hyperthyroidism, CKD, IBD — all extremely common in middle-aged and senior cats). These owners spend thousands per year on vet care and pet food, are highly emotionally invested, and will pay for anything that makes care feel less chaotic. Skews female, 28–55, disposable income.

**Monetization:** Freemium mobile app (iOS + Android). Free tier: up to 2 cats, basic reminders. Pro tier: $3.99/month or $29.99/year, unlimited cats, health log, weight chart, vet record storage, medication history export as PDF for vet appointments. At 500 Pro subscribers, that's $15,000/year — modest, but the App Store audience compounds via search and word of mouth in cat communities.

**MVP scope:** Add cats with photos. Per cat: feeding schedule with checkboxes (resets daily), medication log with dosage and time, weight log with a simple chart. Household dashboard showing today's status across all cats at a glance — done/not done. Vet appointment reminder. Push notifications. Does NOT include vet telehealth, does NOT integrate with any external pet health service, no social features, no breed database.

**Distribution:** r/cats, r/catcare, r/FELV, r/diabeticpets (a niche but passionate community that desperately needs this). Also TheCatSite.com forums, which are massive and full of exactly the owners managing complicated multi-cat care. Facebook groups for diabetic cat owners are extremely active and share tools constantly. One post with a genuine personal story about why you built it will reach thousands of the right people.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The multi-cat complexity angle is too small for Chewy or PetSmart to build a dedicated tool for, but it's large enough to support a bootstrapped product. General pet app makers optimize for the one-dog family. A developer who actually has cats — and builds the app with the kind of specific detail that only comes from lived experience (per-cat insulin tracking, the ability to note "refused food this morning") — will earn the community's trust in a way a generic app can't.

**Biggest risk:** Discoverability on the App Store is brutal for new apps, and cat owners have to *find* this before word of mouth can work. The mitigation is going community-first before the App Store submission — build the waitlist in forums, not in the store. The secondary risk is scope creep: the moment you launch, users will ask for vet telehealth, food databases, and insurance integration. Saying no is a feature.
