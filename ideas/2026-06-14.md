**Idea 1: SyncKit**

**Problem:** Bedroom producers who want to license music to sync agencies (TV, ads, trailers, games) spend hours manually prepping submission packages. Every agency wants something slightly different: stems labeled "FULL MIX / NO VOCAL / DRUMS ONLY," specific bit depths and sample rates, BPM and key embedded in filenames, metadata CSVs, all zipped to spec. There's no tooling for this — producers either do it by hand every time or skip licensing entirely because the overhead kills momentum. Existing DAW export workflows have no concept of "submission-ready."

**Target buyer:** Bedroom producers who have finished tracks and are actively submitting to sync libraries — specifically people working with Musicbed, Artlist, Epidemic Sound, or boutique sync agencies. They're releasing on Bandcamp or DistroKid already but want the sync revenue stream. They've invested real money in their setup and treat it as a side business.

**Monetization:** $12/month subscription or $89/year. Targets producers making or trying to make real licensing income — they'll pay to remove friction. At 200 subscribers: ~$2,400/month ARR. A lifetime deal launch on AppSumo or Product Hunt could front-load $8–15k.

**MVP scope:** User defines an "agency profile" (naming convention templates, required stems, target format, metadata fields). They drop in a folder of already-exported raw stems. SyncKit renames files per the template, runs ffmpeg under the hood to convert to target sample rate/bit depth, generates a metadata CSV from a form the producer fills in once per track (BPM, key, mood tags, ISRC), and outputs a submission-ready zip. Does NOT integrate with DAWs directly, does NOT auto-export stems from projects, does NOT manage licensing deals or contracts.

**Distribution:** r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, r/edmproduction, the Sync Up podcast community, YouTube comments on sync licensing tutorial channels (Musformation, Multiplier). Cold DM producers who post "just submitted to [agency]" on Twitter/X. A free "agency spec database" page (what each major agency requires) becomes an SEO wedge.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** This is too small and too workflow-specific for Adobe or any DAW vendor to care about. The value is in knowing the exact quirks of 15–20 specific agency submission portals — knowledge that changes constantly and requires someone plugged into the community. A solo dev *in* that community can update specs and add new agency profiles in an afternoon.

**Biggest risk:** The pool of producers actively pursuing sync (versus just dreaming about it) may be smaller than it feels online. Validate by finding 10 people to pay $89 before writing a line of code.

**Idea 2: LoadBench**

**Problem:** Handloaders — people who load their own rifle and pistol ammunition — manage genuinely complex data: powder charges down to 0.1gr increments, seating depths, primer types, brass headspace measurements, velocity strings from a chronograph, and accuracy results at multiple distances. They're iterating toward a "pet load" across dozens of calibers and components. Today this lives in paper notebooks, Excel sheets, or aging Windows-only apps (Reloading Assistant, QuickLOAD) that look like they were built in 2004 and haven't shipped an update since. The data is siloed from the range session, so connecting a powder charge to the velocity it produced to the group size it printed requires flipping between three places.

**Target buyer:** Serious precision rifle shooters and competition handgun shooters who reload — specifically people who own a progressive press (Dillon 650/750, RCBS, Hornady), have 3+ calibers in rotation, and track their data because accuracy matters to them. These people spend $1,000–$3,000/year on components. They are not casual.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase, $45 for desktop app (Mac + Windows). No subscription — reloaders distrust "cloud dependency" on something safety-critical. 500 sales = $22,500. Add a $10 one-time mobile companion (range session entry from phone) as upsell.

**MVP scope:** Load recipe database (caliber, bullet, powder, charge, primer, brass, COAL, trim length). Component inventory with lot number tracking and low-stock alerts. Range session log linked to a recipe: date, temperature, firearm, distance, chronograph string (import CSV from Magnetospeed/LabRadar), group size, notes. Cost-per-round calculator. PDF export for a given recipe's full development history. Does NOT include built-in load data from published manuals (liability). Does NOT sync to cloud.

**Distribution:** r/reloading, r/longrange, r/precision_rimfire, The High Road forums, 24hourcampfire.com, Sniper's Hide. Sponsor a single mid-size YouTube reloading channel for $200–400. The community is tight-knit and vocal — one honest review thread on Brian Enos Forums can drive hundreds of sales.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The category is genuinely abandoned. Existing apps are pre-mobile-era abandonware. Big companies (Hornady, RCBS) sell equipment, not software. The dev here has domain knowledge — they know what a seating depth increment means, what a bolt-face runout measurement is, why lot numbers matter. That credibility closes sales in a community that sniffs out outsiders instantly.

**Biggest risk:** Reloaders are conservative and habitual. Many genuinely prefer paper notebooks for perceived safety/legal reasons. The pitch has to be "this is your notebook, but searchable and with math" — never "cloud backup of your load data."

**Idea 3: ReviewPulse**

**Problem:** Indie developers who've shipped a game on Steam are flying blind on what players actually complain about. Valve's dashboard shows review scores and volume but zero semantic breakdown. Devs read reviews manually, which means their gut impression of "players hate the controls" is based on the three reviews they happened to skim. Meanwhile, a patch priority list, a store page rewrite, or a "what's your next game" decision are all downstream of actually understanding what 300 reviews are saying thematically. No tool connects Steam review data to actionable development decisions.

**Target buyer:** Indie developers with a shipped Steam game sitting between 50 and 2,000 reviews — enough signal to analyze, small enough that a big team or budget isn't involved. Specifically devs who are actively patching, planning DLC, or deciding whether to port to console. They're already paying for analytics tools (GameAnalytics, etc.) and understand software subscriptions.

**Monetization:** $19/month SaaS, one game per subscription. Multi-game plan at $39/month. A dev with a live game earning any revenue will pay $19/month to understand it better. 150 subscribers = $2,850/month. Annual prepay discount drives commitment.

**MVP scope:** Connect your Steam game via App ID. Pull all reviews via Steam's public API. Run them through OpenAI embeddings/classification to auto-cluster into topics (performance, controls, story, length, price, bugs, UI). Show topic frequency, average sentiment per topic, trend over time (did "performance" complaints spike after your last patch?), and a filtered view per topic so you can read all "controls" reviews in one place. Export to CSV. Does NOT integrate with GitHub, Jira, or any dev pipeline. Does NOT support Epic, itch.io, or console stores in v1.

**Distribution:** r/gamedev, r/indiegaming developer side, IndieDB, the Indie Game Business Discord, Mastodon's gamedev community. Cold outreach to devs whose Steam pages show recent patches (visible in update history — they're actively maintaining, which means they care). Write one blog post: "We analyzed 10,000 Steam reviews across 20 indie games — here's what players actually complain about." That piece does SEO and Twitter work for months.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Valve will never build this — surfacing "your game's controls are consistently criticized" isn't in their incentive structure. Unity and Epic analytics track behavior, not sentiment. The insight this provides is meaningful to a solo dev in a way it isn't to a publisher with 50 titles. A solo dev who knows the indie game world can write copy and position this in language that resonates exactly.

**Biggest risk:** The Steam API throttles and changes without warning. Also, LLM classification costs money per-analysis, so pricing needs to account for large review volumes. Cap free tier at 200 reviews, charge for full pulls.

**Idea 4: Canon**

**Problem:** Self-publishing sci-fi and fantasy authors writing multi-book series have no purpose-built tool for continuity management. They track characters, timelines, invented technology, faction politics, and world geography across 300,000+ words in multiple volumes — using Scrivener's flat note cards, a Notion database that becomes unmaintainable, or nothing at all. The result: a character's eye color changes between books, a ship's travel time is inconsistent, a technology that doesn't exist yet in book two gets referenced. Continuity errors are the #1 complaint in 1-star reviews of self-published genre series. World Anvil exists but it's built for tabletop RPG game masters, is web-only, and costs $11–25/month for a bloated feature set writers don't need.

**Target buyer:** Self-published sci-fi and fantasy authors on their second or third book in a series — specifically people making real money through Kindle Unlimited and direct sales (the "20 Books to 50K" crowd). They think of their writing as a business. They have disposable income and pay for Scrivener, ProWritingAid, and Vellum without flinching.

**Monetization:** $49 one-time purchase (desktop app, local-first). Optional $4/month for encrypted cloud sync between devices. The local-first angle is a direct competitive jab at World Anvil's subscription. 300 sales = $14,700. A "series starter bundle" could include templates for common sci-fi universe archetypes.

**MVP scope:** Entity types: Characters, Locations, Factions, Technologies, Events, Books/Chapters. Each entity is a rich-text wiki article. Entities link to each other (Character → Faction, Event → Location → Character). Visual timeline view with events plotted across a custom date system (for invented calendars). Contradiction checker: flag if the same entity has conflicting attributes across articles (eye color, rank, etc.). Full offline, local SQLite storage, exports to Markdown. Does NOT generate content with AI. Does NOT have a "collaborate with co-author" mode. Does NOT sync to Scrivener or Word.

**Distribution:** The 20 Books to 50K Facebook group (100k+ members, highly active). r/selfpublish, r/worldbuilding. Kboards Writers' Café. Reach out to mid-list self-pub authors (10k–50k Twitter/newsletter followers) for review copies — one "I switched from World Anvil" post from a respected author is worth $5k in ads. A YouTube demo showing the contradiction checker finding a real error in a sample manuscript will go viral in that community.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** World Anvil is run by a small team optimizing for tabletop RPG revenue. Scrivener has deliberately avoided going deep on worldbuilding. The fiction writer's needs (timeline, continuity, export to prose) are different from the game master's needs (stat blocks, encounter tables). A solo dev who reads sci-fi can build the exact artifact this audience wants without committee bloat.

**Biggest risk:** Writers are famously reluctant to change their workflow. "I've been doing this in Notion for 3 years" is a strong objection. The contradiction checker has to visibly catch something real in the demo — that's the wedge that breaks inertia.

**Idea 5: StageScore**

**Problem:** Competitive practical shooters (USPSA, IDPA, 3-Gun) who are serious about improving their classification have no good tool for tracking what actually matters: stage-by-stage performance trends, hit factor history against classifiers, equipment setup logs linked to match outcomes, and dry fire training volume versus match results. USPSA publishes classifier scores publicly, but interpreting your own trajectory — "am I actually progressing toward A-class or just having lucky matches?" — requires manually pulling data and doing math most shooters skip. The dominant solution is a paper notebook and vibes.

**Target buyer:** USPSA/IDPA B-class and A-class competitors who shoot 8–15 matches per year and train seriously between them — the kind of shooter who owns a dedicated competition gun, tracks their gear with a torque wrench, and watches Steve Anderson or Ben Stoeger drill videos. They spend $400–800/year on match fees and ammo alone. Software is a rounding error.

**Monetization:** $6/month or $55/year subscription. The annual plan framing ("less than one match fee") closes the value question immediately. 200 subscribers = $11,000 ARR. Upsell: $15 one-time for an "equipment vault" module (detailed gear log with maintenance history, spring replacement cycles, zero records).

**MVP scope:** Manual match entry (match name, date, division, classification, total points, total time, hit factor per stage). Classifier tracker: pulls your public USPSA classifier history via the public results data, shows your current percentage and what hit factors you need to hit to move up. Dry fire session log (drills, duration, notes). Trend charts: hit factor over time per stage type (standards vs. field courses). Does NOT integrate with shot timers directly. Does NOT pull non-classifier match results automatically (USPSA's API for that is restricted). Does NOT have a social/leaderboard feature in v1.

**Distribution:** r/USPSA, r/competitionshooting, Brian Enos Forums (the most active practical shooting community online, with a dedicated "gear and equipment" subforum), the Practical Shooting Training Group Facebook group. Sponsor a thread on Enos Forums — the community there will beta test aggressively and give brutal, useful feedback. One video of the classifier trend chart showing someone's path from C to B class is the entire marketing pitch.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The USPSA organization has no interest in building member analytics software — they run a sport, not a SaaS business. The match scoring apps (PractiScore) track raw results but have no analytical layer. This is too small for any company to bother with, but the target buyers are exactly the kind of obsessive who will pay $55/year and tell their squad about it at the next club match.

**Biggest risk:** USPSA classifier data is public but scraping it at scale may hit rate limits or policy changes. Validate that the public results endpoint is stable before building the auto-import feature. If it breaks, the app falls back to manual entry — make sure that's still useful enough to retain subscribers.
