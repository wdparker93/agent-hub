# Weekly App Ideas — June 28, 2026

**Idea 1: Handload Lab**

**Problem:** Precision rifle shooters who reload their own ammunition are obsessive data collectors. They log powder charge, bullet, primer, brass, OAL, chronograph strings, and group sizes for every load they develop — and then they lose it all in a spreadsheet maze. When they want to find their best load for a new barrel or compare lot-to-lot powder variation, they're ctrl-F'ing through Excel. QuickLOAD does pressure prediction, not record-keeping. Phone apps exist but they're essentially glorified notepads with no statistics. The analysis layer — extreme spread trends, SD across temperatures, load-vs-accuracy correlation — simply doesn't exist in any modern tool.

**Target buyer:** Precision Rifle Series competitors, long-range hunters, and dedicated benchrest shooters who reload their own ammo. People who own a Labradar or MagnetoSpeed and already think in standard deviations. Not the guy reloading .38 Special for fun — the person spending $600 on a Prometheus powder measure and keeping a lab notebook.

**Monetization:** One-time purchase desktop app (Windows + Mac), $59. No subscription needed — reloaders distrust SaaS for something this personal, and a one-time purchase fits the "serious tool" framing. Optional $15 upgrade for cloud backup/sync. 600 sales in year one = $35K, realistic given the size and spending habits of r/reloading (260K members) and Sniper's Hide (300K+ registered).

**MVP scope:** Create and save load recipes with all relevant fields. Log shooting sessions against a recipe (date, temperature, range distance, shot string velocities). Auto-calculate ES and SD per session. Side-by-side load comparison view. PDF export of any load's history. Does NOT do: pressure prediction, integration with chronograph hardware, public load sharing, mobile sync, or cartridge database auto-fill.

**Distribution:** r/reloading, r/longrange, r/PrecisionRifle. Post a "I built this because I was sick of Excel" thread with screenshots — these communities respond strongly to that framing. YouTube comment sections on Applied Ballistics and Garabaldi videos always have "what software do you use?" threads. Sniper's Hide forum. A free trial that exports watermarked PDFs converts lurkers.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Hornady and Sierra make reloading manuals and pressure software, not session logging tools — it's beneath their product line. The niche is too small for a funded startup. A solo dev can iterate weekly based on direct community feedback, price it honestly, and build exactly the 15 features serious reloaders actually want instead of 150 features a product manager imagined.

**Biggest risk:** The most engaged reloaders are already comfortable in Excel and will explain in detail why they don't need your app. The "wow moment" has to be visible in a 30-second GIF — probably the SD trend graph over 10 sessions. Without that hook, it's a solutions-looking-for-a-problem launch.

**Idea 2: PatchKit**

**Problem:** ROM hackers — people who create fan modifications of classic games — are building software without software tools. A serious ROM hack can take 12–18 months of work. They're managing it with: Lunar IPS (released in 2003, Windows-only, no project concept), a folder of text files, screenshots dumped into Discord, and handwritten notes about what changed between versions. There's no way to log "v0.4 — fixed the softlock in Aquas, changed boss HP values, broke save states on BizHawk." ROMhacking.net's submission process requires a readme, a version history, and compatibility notes — all assembled manually. This is a developer community that has never been given developer tooling.

**Target buyer:** Active ROM hackers who release publicly on ROMhacking.net — specifically those working on multi-year translation or gameplay rebalance projects for SNES, GBA, and PS1 titles. People serious enough to have a project thread and a Discord server for their hack, but not so resourced that they've built their own workflow tools.

**Monetization:** Free core (apply IPS/BPS/UPS patches, works as a modern Lunar IPS replacement) drives discovery and goodwill. $24 one-time unlock for the project management layer. The free tool becomes the recommended patch applier in tutorials; the paid layer converts the hackers themselves. 400 paying users = $9,600. Small, but this is a side project, not a lifestyle business — and the free tier builds reputation that opens consulting or commission opportunities in the community.

**MVP scope:** A clean desktop GUI that applies IPS, BPS, and UPS patches to a base ROM (using existing open-source libraries, no reinventing the wheel). Paid layer: a "Project" object that stores a base ROM hash (not the ROM itself — legally clean), session notes per version, a changelog editor, and a readme generator pre-formatted for ROMhacking.net submissions. Does NOT do: hex editing, tile/graphics editing, emulator integration, or team collaboration.

**Distribution:** ROMhacking.net forums and Discord, r/romhacking, YouTube — every ROM hacking tutorial on YouTube gets comments asking "what tool is this?" Create a 3-minute "replace Lunar IPS forever" video. The free tier handles its own distribution; word spreads fast in small hobby communities.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** The addressable market is too small for any company to care about, which is exactly why the tooling is stuck in 2003. A developer who actually plays and appreciates ROM hacks can engage authentically — not as a vendor, but as a community member who scratched their own itch. That authenticity is the entire go-to-market.

**Biggest risk:** ROM hackers are themselves developers and DIY by nature — high resistance to paying for something they could theoretically build. The free tier must be genuinely better than Lunar IPS, not marginally better, or there's no funnel into the paid tier.

**Idea 3: Litter Registry**

**Problem:** Small-scale cat breeders (hobby breeders with 2–8 breeding cats) run what amounts to a small business: managing waitlists of 20–40 people per litter, tracking health testing records (HCM scans, blood typing, genetic panels) for each cat, logging vaccination schedules, handling deposits, and producing paperwork for TICA/CFA registration. They do all of this across a tangle of Google Sheets, Gmail labels, and paper folders. Professional cattery software exists (e.g., Breedmate) but it's Windows-only legacy software with a 1999 UI, priced at $200+ for a perpetual license that still doesn't have mobile access or a decent waitlist view. Modern breeders are running their cattery out of their phone.

**Target buyer:** TICA- or CFA-registered hobby breeders producing 2–5 litters per year — specifically those selling kittens at $1,500–$4,000 each who take registration and health testing seriously. This is not the backyard breeder. This is the person who knows the difference between N/N and N/HCM and posts health test results publicly on their cattery website.

**Monetization:** $12/month or $99/year SaaS. Upsell at $18/month for a public-facing "Available Kittens" page hosted on your domain that pulls from their records (eliminates the need for a separate website). 150 subscribers at blended $110/year = $16,500 ARR. 400 subscribers = ~$44K. Breeder Facebook groups alone reach tens of thousands of active breeders.

**MVP scope:** Cat profiles (each breeding cat: health tests with dates and results, vaccination log, notes). Litter records: parents, DOB, kitten count, sex, color, outcome (kept/sold/pet home). Waitlist per litter: contact name, email, deposit status, preferences, notes. Basic calendar view of upcoming health test renewals and vet appointments. Mobile-responsive web app. Does NOT do: automated email sequences, invoice generation, registry submission, photo galleries, or the public kitten page (that's v2).

**Distribution:** Cat breeder Facebook groups are enormous and extremely active — groups like "TICA Breeders" and breed-specific groups (Maine Coon, Ragdoll, Bengal) have 10K–50K members each. Direct outreach: DM 20 breeders whose cattery websites clearly show they're doing this in a spreadsheet. A "I'm building this, would you beta test it?" post in the right group can land 50 beta users in a week.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Legacy vendors aren't watching UX trends. A clean, fast, mobile-first web app that does 80% of what they need at half the price wins on aesthetics alone. The community is reachable through very specific, low-cost channels, and breeders make recommendations to each other constantly. One champion breeder in a popular Facebook group is worth 30 cold leads.

**Biggest risk:** Breeders are privacy-sensitive (customer data, cat pedigree data) and slow to trust web-based tools. "I don't want my kitten waitlist in the cloud" is a real objection. Needs prominent data export, a clear privacy policy, and ideally a one-click CSV download visible on the first screen.

**Idea 4: Storefront Ledger**

**Problem:** Indie game developers releasing on multiple storefronts — Steam, itch.io, GOG, Humble Store — have no unified view of their business. Steam's Steamworks dashboard shows Steam data. itch.io shows itch data. Comparing them requires exporting CSVs, reconciling different date formats, and building pivot tables. Questions like "which platform drove more sales during my last sale event?", "what's my trailing 90-day refund rate across all platforms?", or "is my GOG revenue actually worth the support burden?" require an afternoon of spreadsheet work instead of a 10-second glance. This is a developer tooling problem that happens to live in the games industry.

**Target buyer:** Solo and small-team indie developers who have shipped at least one game on two or more storefronts and are actively managing their back catalog. Specifically those running regular sale participation and trying to make data-driven decisions about where to focus their marketing energy.

**Monetization:** Freemium SaaS. Free tier: connect one storefront, 60-day history, basic revenue chart. Paid: $9/month or $79/year, all storefronts, full history, weekly email digest, anomaly alerts ("Steam sales spike 3x vs. last week"). 200 paying developers = $18,400/year ARR. Not life-changing alone, but highly retentive — once your sales history is in a tool, you don't leave.

**MVP scope:** Steam integration via Steamworks financial report CSV upload (no API key dance required for v1). itch.io integration via their OAuth API. Unified revenue-over-time chart, broken out by platform. Units sold, gross revenue, net revenue (after platform cut), refunds. Simple threshold-based email alert. Does NOT do: ad spend correlation, in-game analytics, social media metrics, GOG/Epic integration (v2), or automatic report ingestion (v2).

**Distribution:** r/gamedev is 1M+ members and constantly discussing business metrics. IndieDB. The #gamedev and #indiedev communities on Twitter/Mastodon. Game Developer Conference Discord servers. Write a public teardown post — "I analyzed 12 months of cross-platform indie sales data and here's what I found" — which demonstrates the product's value while driving SEO and community attention. The free tier does the rest.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** Amplitude and Mixpanel serve app developers, not storefront revenue. GameAnalytics focuses on in-game telemetry. No one has built the "sales dashboard for indie devs" as a standalone product because it's too niche for a VC-backed analytics company. A solo dev with genuine indie game interest can build credibility in the community that a faceless SaaS vendor can't.

**Biggest risk:** Storefront data access is fragile. Steam's Steamworks reports require a shipped game to access and the format can change without notice. Itch.io's API has historically been limited. If the data pipeline breaks, churn follows immediately. V1 must treat data ingestion reliability as the core product, not a backend detail.

**Idea 5: Lightcone**

**Problem:** Writers and game designers building hard sci-fi settings have a consistency problem that no worldbuilding tool addresses: physics. If your story spans multiple star systems, characters in different locations literally cannot know the same things at the same time — news travels at the speed of light, which means a colony on Proxima Centauri is always 4.2 years behind Earth on current events. Tracking "who knows what, and when" across an interstellar timeline becomes a continuity nightmare that Notion, Obsidian, and World Anvil cannot help with because they have no concept of physics. Hard sci-fi readers — who've absorbed three Alastair Reynolds novels and will notice — will find every mistake. Authors currently manage this on whiteboards and custom spreadsheets. Nobody has built the tool because you have to actually care about the problem to understand it.

**Target buyer:** Hard sci-fi novelists working on series with interstellar or relativistic settings — specifically those with an existing readership or newsletter, likely self-published or small press, who've already proven they'll invest in their craft. Secondary: narrative designers at indie sci-fi game studios (think the team making a Mass Effect-inspired RPG in a 3-person studio). People who've specifically complained that "no worldbuilding software handles FTL communication delays."

**Monetization:** $8/month or $65/year. A "Studio" tier at $16/month unlocks collaboration for up to 3 users (small game studios). Free tier: 2 star systems, single timeline, no export. Paid: unlimited systems, full timeline, consistency report export, shareable read-only timeline link. 250 subscribers at blended $75/year = $18,750 ARR. Niche but extremely low churn — your world data lives here.

**MVP scope:** Star system database with real or fictional 3D coordinates (import from HYG star catalog or enter manually). Event log: add an event at a location and date. Automatic light-travel-time calculation between any two systems. Character location tracker: place a character at a system at a given date. Consistency warning engine: flag any scene where a character references an event that, based on light travel time, they cannot yet know about. Does NOT do: procedural universe generation, FTL rules customization beyond "light speed," Scrivener integration, in-app writing, or real orbital mechanics.

**Distribution:** r/worldbuilding, r/scifiwriting, r/HardSciFi. The Writing Excuses podcast community. NaNoWriMo forums in November. Write a Substack or Medium post titled "The light cone problem: why your sci-fi novel is probably inconsistent" — it's genuinely useful content that explains the product's value to exactly the right audience. One or two retweets from mid-tier sci-fi authors with engaged audiences does the rest. The free tier's 2-system limit is useful enough to demonstrate the concept.

**Why a solo dev wins here:** World Anvil, the market leader in worldbuilding SaaS, is built for fantasy and general fiction — their team has no particular reason to care about relativistic consistency engines. This feature requires genuine domain enthusiasm to specify correctly. A solo dev who's read the right books can build exactly what the community needs and speak to it authentically. The community is small enough to engage personally; the passion is high enough that early users will recruit others.

**Biggest risk:** The hard-sci-fi audience who truly needs this is smaller than the general sci-fi audience that would browse it but never pay. Scope discipline is critical — resist the pull toward becoming a general worldbuilding tool. The moment you add a "races" tab or a "magic system" section, you're competing with World Anvil on its home turf instead of owning the physics-consistency niche you actually invented.
